# The Musical Beat Map — corpus × story-structure guides

*Research doc. Companion to TEMPLATE-PLAN.md (§4 scene prompts) and SPEC §15
Story DNA. Source: the 81-show corpus, 1,484 classified songs (1943–2026,
Broadway + West End + continental Europe, hits AND flops). Data in `corpus/`.*

**Stability note:** every beat-window leader below held unchanged as the
corpus grew from 797 to 1,484 songs — the map has converged. §6 tests it
against a flop control group.

The question: when the popular beat systems are laid over where musicals
actually put their songs, what lines up — and what does the musical do
differently? Guides consulted: **Snyder** (Save the Cat BS2), **Field**
(paradigm: PP1 25% / Midpoint 50% / PP2 75%), **Vogler** (Hero's Journey),
**Harmon** (story circle), **Bell** (mirror moment), **Truby** (7 steps,
self-revelation), and the app's own **Wells/Baugh 7-beat chiasmus with the
Chamberlain catch** (Story DNA §15).

## 2 · The headline alignments

### a. The app's four lanes ARE Field's paradigm — measured

From the carded shelf: **lane 1 ends at 28% · intermission at 56% · lane 2B
ends at 77%.** Field says 25 / 50 / 75. The lane spine was already the
three-act paradigm; the corpus just proved it:

```
lane 1  = Act One        (to Plot Point 1, ~28%)
lane 2A = Act Two-a      (to the Midpoint,  ~56%)
lane 2B = Act Two-b      (to Plot Point 2,  ~77%)
lane 3  = Act Three
```

Harmon's circle drops straight on: 1 = You/Need · 2A = Go/Search ·
2B = Find/Take · 3 = Return/Change.

### b. The intermission IS the Midpoint — not the act break of a play

A musical's Act One = a film's Acts 1 + 2a. The interval lands on Snyder's
Midpoint / Bell's mirror / the chiasmus nucleus (56–58%), NOT on All Is Lost.
Consequence: the musical's second act must compress Bad Guys Close In → All Is
Lost → Dark Night → Resurrection → Final Image into ~40% of the runtime —
which is exactly why Act 2 leans on short reprises and why the corpus shows
fewer, tighter A2 songs (1.36:1).

### c. The mirror moment is externalized as the Act One finale

In the 46–54% mirror window, the **act finale dominates** (26 of 88 songs).
Novels put the protagonist alone at a mirror; musicals put the choice on the
curtain line — *Defying Gravity*, *One Day More*, *Why We Build the Wall*,
*La Vie Bohème*, *A Little Priest*. For Story DNA: the chiasmus **midpoint
nucleus** and the **intermission cliff** are the same object. The beat-4 panel
is, in practice, describing the Act One finale. The landmark exception proves
the mapping: **Carousel's act finale IS a soliloquy** — Billy alone at the
literal mirror moment, 7½ minutes, at 48%.

### d. The I Want is Theme-Stated-through-Debate, sung

The I Want spans 5–22% (avg 18%), covering Snyder's Theme Stated → Catalyst →
Debate as one continuous musical gesture — the debate is *resolved by singing
it*. Cold-open shows (Annie, Spring Awakening) compress further: the I Want IS
the opening image. Crossing the threshold follows the song.

### e. Comedy spikes exactly at the Break Into Two

The 22–28% window is the only window comedy *leads* (20%). Colin's editorial
rule — "the comedy begins in the new world, after the Break Into Two" — is
empirically the corpus's rule. (Golden Age patter like *With a Little Bit of
Luck* is the setup-era exception.)

### f. All Is Lost is a ballad; the Dark Night has a name in the program

72–80% is the lament zone (ballad leads at 22%: *On My Own*, *Little Brother*,
*Days and Days*). The Act-2 soliloquy cluster (avg 74%, peaking 77–86%:
*Javert's Suicide*, *What Would You Do?*, *Edges of the World*, *The Ladies
Who Lunch*) is the **Dark Night of the Soul with a character's name on it**.
The template's Act-3 soliloquy slot is this beat.

### g. The eleven o'clock number = Resurrection = self-revelation = the A-ha

86–94% is owned by the eleven (26% of the window; function avg 88%, range
80–95). Vogler's Resurrection, Truby's self-revelation, and the chiasmus
**A-ha that inverts the Chamberlain catch** are all the same event in a
musical — and it is a *song*, 22 of 30 times a solo: *Being Alive*, *Cabaret*,
*Memory* ('82 winner), *Epic III*, *My Days*. For the Story DNA validator: a
show with no solo in the 80–95% band is probably missing its beat-6.

### h. The reprise is the chiasmus made audible

Reprises run 13 in A1 vs 34 in A2 — material from the **rising side returns
transformed on the falling side**. That is Wells/Baugh mirroring as an
auditory device: beat 2↔6 and 3↔5 pairs can literally share a melody
(*Wait for Me* → *Wait for Me (Rep)*, *Runs in the Family* → Rep). And the
frame pair 1↔7 is the **bookend reprise**: Hadestown (*Road to Hell*), The
Outsiders (*Tulsa '67*), Cabaret (*Willkommen*), Sweeney (*The Ballad*),
Company-style title returns. When Story DNA asks "does the ending mirror the
opening?", the musical's native answer is: *does the finale reprise it?*

## 3 · The 7-beat chiasmus, scored (for Story DNA §15)

| Chiasmus beat | Corpus song function | Measured position |
|---|---|---|
| 1 · Hook | opening | 0–5% |
| 2 · Threshold (+ catch) | iwant → the Break | 5–28% |
| 3 · rising pinch | villain (44%) · love-as-B-story | 28–50% |
| 4 · **Nucleus / mirror** | **act finale = intermission cliff** | 50–58% |
| 5 · falling mirror of 3 | drive · ballad · **reprises of 3's material** | 58–77% |
| 6 · **A-ha** (inverts the catch) | soliloquy (74%) → **eleven (88%)** | 77–94% |
| 7 · Ending mirrors 1 | finaleultimo, often a **bookend reprise** | 94–100% |

The truth line rises through beats 1–3 in charm/love keys; the want line falls
through 5–6 in ballad/drive keys; the nucleus is the loudest bar of the night.

## 4 · Regional traditions (the cross-culture check)

| Tradition | n | A1:A2 | A1 share | Character |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Broadway book musical | 42 | 1.40 | 59% | frontloaded Act 1, big split |
| West End | 10 | **1.16** | **54%** | flat, sung-through-leaning |
| Continental Europe | 2 | 1.22 | 54% | tableau/spectacle, flat |

The newest pair (Mrs. Doubtfire '21 at 52%, Paddington '25 at 56%) both sit
on the modern flat profile — the 21st-century floor holds across traditions.

The **frontloaded Act One is an American book-musical trait**. The British
sung-through line (Evita 50%, JCS 49%, Blood Brothers 51%) balances its acts
because there's no book to frontload — and even British book shows run level
(Matilda is A2-heavy at 45%). Notre-Dame de Paris builds Act 1 from ~28 short
tableaux — the French spectacle inherits opera's number-chain, not the book
musical's scene/song alternation. The beat *sequence* is identical across all
three traditions — only the intermission's position moves. The beats are the
invariant; the act break is the dialect.

## 5 · Three more lenses, briefly

- **Frank Daniel's 8-sequence method:** corpus average ≈ 18 songs/show =
  **2.3 songs per sequence** — a sequence is a scene + two-to-three songs.
  The 8 sequences pair off into our 4 lanes exactly (2 per lane).
- **Kishōtenketsu** (ki-shō-ten-ketsu, the East Asian 4-part non-conflict
  form): maps onto the 4 lanes with **ten (the twist) = the post-intermission
  turn** — lane 2B as recontextualization rather than escalation. Useful lens
  for gentler shows (Come From Away and Maybe Happy Ending both read more
  kishōtenketsu than Snyder).
- **McKee's five-part:** inciting incident ≈ end of the I Want zone (~20%);
  progressive complications = 2A; crisis = the act break; climax = the
  eleven; resolution = finale ultimo. Nothing new beyond Snyder/Field — McKee
  and the corpus agree because they're describing the same animal.

## 6 · The survivorship test — what this map can and cannot promise

The corpus was 100% survivors until a five-show flop control group was added
(Merrily '81 · Chess '88 · Carrie '88 · Big '96 · Bonnie & Clyde '11), scored
against the hit corpus's function-position means:

| | Beat-conformity (mean deviation) | Act finale in place | Finale ultimo in place |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Flops** (n=5) | **16.2%** | 5/5 | 5/5 |
| **Hits** (n=63) | **16.8%** | 92% | 95% |

**Flops follow the map as faithfully as hits** — Bonnie & Clyde is one of the
five most-conforming shows in the entire corpus, and Hamlisch's Sweet Smell
is textbook. Meanwhile the *least*-conforming shows include Gypsy, Once, and
Jesus Christ Superstar — all hits.

The conclusion, stated plainly: **this map is the grammar of the form, not a
recipe for success.** Structure is necessary-but-not-sufficient. What the map
(and the Story DNA validator built on it) can honestly promise: it tells a
writer when their shape is *unusual*, so deviation is a choice rather than an
accident. It cannot tell them whether the show will work — flops die of
execution, tone, and material, not of beat placement. (Merrily is the
poignant case: even reversed in time, its beats sit within 15% of the norm —
the eleven o'clock, *Our Time*, lands at 92% and is *hopeful*, because
backwards.)

## 7 · Product hooks

- **Scene-card prompts (TEMPLATE-PLAN §4):** if the scaffold ships with light
  prompts, use the beat names at their measured homes —
  lane 1: "Ordinary world" · "Catalyst"; 2A: "Break into two" · "Midpoint
  approach"; 2B: "All is lost"; 3: "Dark night" (before the soliloquy slot).
- **Story DNA phase-2 validator** gets measurable checks: no I Want by 30% ·
  nucleus not coinciding with the act break (~56–58%) · no solo in the 80–95%
  band (missing A-ha) · no A1 material reprised in A2 (silent chiasmus) ·
  finale that neither reprises nor inverts the opening (open frame).
- **Board beat-chips (later):** the lane header already knows its Field/Harmon
  identity; a subtle label ("to PP1 · ~28%") would cost nothing.
